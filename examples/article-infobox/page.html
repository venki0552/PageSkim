Lighthouse of Alexandria

The Lighthouse of Alexandria, sometimes called the Pharos of Alexandria, was a lighthouse built by the Ptolemaic Kingdom of Ancient Egypt during the reign of Ptolemy II Philadelphus. Construction is generally dated between about 297 and 283 BC. It stood on the eastern tip of the island of Pharos at the entrance to Alexandria's Great Harbour, and estimates of its overall height range around 100 metres. For many centuries it was one of the tallest man-made structures in the world, and it was counted among the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World.

Badly damaged by three earthquakes between 956 and 1323 AD, it became an abandoned ruin. It was the third-longest surviving ancient wonder, after the Mausoleum at Halicarnassus and the still-extant Great Pyramid of Giza. In 1480 the last of its remnant stones were used to build the Citadel of Qaitbay on the same site.

Origin

Pharos was a small island just off the coast of Alexandria, connected to the city by a man-made causeway called the Heptastadion, which thereby formed one side of the harbour. Because the Egyptian coast is flat and lacked the kind of landmark used at the time for navigation, a marker of some sort at the mouth of the harbour was considered necessary — a function the lighthouse was initially designed to serve. Use of the building as a lighthouse, with a fire and reflective mirrors at the top, is thought to date to around the first century AD.

The construction is commonly attributed to Sostratus of Cnidus, though ancient sources disagree on whether he was the architect or the financial sponsor of the project. The monument cost 800 talents of silver according to Pliny the Elder — an enormous sum, roughly a tenth of the treasury Ptolemy I held at his accession.

Height and description

Descriptions by ancient and medieval travellers agree that the lighthouse rose in three tapering stages: a square lower section with a central core, an octagonal middle section, and a circular upper section topped by a large statue, probably of Zeus Soter or Helios. Arab travellers who visited between the tenth and fourteenth centuries recorded measurements that, converted carefully, suggest a total height of just over 100 metres including the foundation platform.

The interior of the lowest stage reportedly contained a wide spiral ramp, allowing fuel for the beacon fire to be hauled up by pack animals. At the summit, a mirror — possibly of polished bronze — reflected sunlight during the day; a fire provided light by night. Some accounts claim the light could be seen up to 47 kilometres away.

Later accounts

The traveller Ibn Battuta visited Alexandria in 1326 and again in 1349. On his first visit he found the lighthouse partly ruined but still possible to enter; on the second, he reported that the ruin was such that entry was no longer possible. His account is among the last eyewitness descriptions of the structure standing to any height.

Destruction

The lighthouse was progressively destroyed by earthquakes in 956, 1303 and 1323 AD. The 1303 Crete earthquake, felt across the eastern Mediterranean, is generally regarded as the event that ended its use. By the mid-fourteenth century the structure was a ruin, and in around 1480 the Mamluk sultan Qaitbay built a fortress on the platform using the fallen stone. The Citadel of Qaitbay still occupies the site today.

Archaeological research

In 1994, a team of archaeologists led by Jean-Yves Empereur documented hundreds of large masonry blocks and statuary fragments on the seabed off Fort Qaitbay, many of which are believed to have fallen from the lighthouse. Subsequent surveys mapped columns, sphinxes and colossal statue fragments, some of which have been raised and displayed. The underwater site was added to UNESCO's tentative list of submerged cultural heritage in Alexandria.

Legacy

The lighthouse gave its name to the architectural type: pharos became the word for lighthouse in Greek, and survives in the French phare, Italian and Spanish faro, Romanian far and Portuguese farol. Minarets of early Egyptian mosques are thought by some scholars to echo its three-stage form, and modern reconstructions of the building have been proposed several times, including a 2015 Egyptian plan to rebuild it near the original site.
