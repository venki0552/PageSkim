Lighthouse of Alexandria

"Pharos" redirects here. For the island, see Pharos (island). For other uses, see Pharos (disambiguation).

The Lighthouse of Alexandria, sometimes called the Pharos of Alexandria, was a lighthouse built by the Ptolemaic Kingdom of Ancient Egypt during the reign of Ptolemy II Philadelphus. Construction is generally dated between about 297 and 283 BC. It stood on the eastern tip of the island of Pharos at the entrance to Alexandria's Great Harbour, and estimates of its overall height range around 100 metres. For many centuries it was one of the tallest man-made structures in the world, and it was counted among the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World.

Badly damaged by three earthquakes between 956 and 1323 AD, it became an abandoned ruin. It was the third-longest surviving ancient wonder, after the Mausoleum at Halicarnassus and the still-extant Great Pyramid of Giza. In 1480 the last of its remnant stones were used to build the Citadel of Qaitbay on the same site.

Origin

Pharos was a small island just off the coast of Alexandria, connected to the city by a man-made causeway called the Heptastadion, which thereby formed one side of the harbour. Because the Egyptian coast is flat and lacked the kind of landmark used at the time for navigation, a marker of some sort at the mouth of the harbour was considered necessary — a function the lighthouse was initially designed to serve. Use of the building as a lighthouse, with a fire and reflective mirrors at the top, is thought to date to around the first century AD.

The construction is commonly attributed to Sostratus of Cnidus, though ancient sources disagree on whether he was the architect or the financial sponsor of the project. The monument cost 800 talents of silver according to Pliny the Elder — an enormous sum, roughly a tenth of the treasury Ptolemy I held at his accession. Legend holds that Sostratus was forbidden by Ptolemy from putting his name on the work, and that he carved a dedication into the masonry anyway, covering it with plaster bearing the king's name; the plaster was meant to weather away and reveal the builder's inscription to posterity.

Construction

The lighthouse was built from large blocks of light-coloured stone — a combination of limestone in the body and granite at points of structural stress, according to the archaeological consensus. The blocks were sealed together with molten lead rather than ordinary mortar, a measure ancient writers explain as protection against the pounding of the waves. Construction on the exposed reef required the platform to be raised above storm level, and the foundation courses were laid directly onto the levelled bedrock of the islet.

Work is thought to have begun under Ptolemy I Soter around 297 BC and to have been completed under his son Ptolemy II Philadelphus around 283 BC, a construction period of roughly twelve to fifteen years. The project drew on the same royal treasury and engineering establishment that produced the Museum and the Library of Alexandria in the same generation, and the three works together made the new capital the most conspicuous city of the Hellenistic world.

Height and description

Descriptions by ancient and medieval travellers agree that the lighthouse rose in three tapering stages: a square lower section with a central core, an octagonal middle section, and a circular upper section topped by a large statue, probably of Zeus Soter or Helios. Arab travellers who visited between the tenth and fourteenth centuries recorded measurements that, converted carefully, suggest a total height of just over 100 metres including the foundation platform.

The interior of the lowest stage reportedly contained a wide spiral ramp, allowing fuel for the beacon fire to be hauled up by pack animals. At the summit, a mirror — possibly of polished bronze — reflected sunlight during the day; a fire provided light by night. Some accounts claim the light could be seen up to 47 kilometres away. The ground stage also housed a garrison and stabling, and travellers describe hundreds of storage rooms opening off the ascending ramp.

Later accounts

The traveller Ibn Battuta visited Alexandria in 1326 and again in 1349. On his first visit he found the lighthouse partly ruined but still possible to enter; on the second, he reported that the ruin was such that entry was no longer possible. His account is among the last eyewitness descriptions of the structure standing to any height.

Destruction

The lighthouse was progressively destroyed by earthquakes in 956, 1303 and 1323 AD. The 956 earthquake cracked the upper stage and brought down the crowning statue; repairs under the Fatimid caliphs restored the tower as a functioning beacon, reportedly adding a small mosque at the summit. The 1303 Crete earthquake, felt across the eastern Mediterranean, is generally regarded as the event that ended its use. By the mid-fourteenth century the structure was a ruin, and in around 1480 the Mamluk sultan Qaitbay built a fortress on the platform using the fallen stone. The Citadel of Qaitbay still occupies the site today.

Archaeological research

In 1994, a team of archaeologists led by Jean-Yves Empereur documented hundreds of large masonry blocks and statuary fragments on the seabed off Fort Qaitbay, many of which are believed to have fallen from the lighthouse. Subsequent surveys mapped columns, sphinxes and colossal statue fragments, some of which have been raised and displayed. More than 3,300 pieces had been catalogued by the project by the end of the decade, including twenty-five sphinxes and fragments of a colossal royal couple in Egyptian style.

The underwater site was added to UNESCO's tentative list of submerged cultural heritage in Alexandria, and proposals have circulated since the late 1990s for an underwater archaeological museum that would make the field of blocks accessible to visitors. Survey work continues, and photogrammetric models of the debris field published in the 2010s have refined estimates of how the tower collapsed.

In ancient and medieval accounts

The lighthouse appears in a remarkably continuous chain of testimony: Strabo and Pliny the Elder describe it in the first centuries BC and AD, Arab geographers including al-Masudi, al-Idrisi and Yaqut record measurements and repairs between the tenth and thirteenth centuries, and Ibn Battuta's fourteenth-century visits bracket its final ruin. Coins minted in Alexandria in the second century AD show the tower with its statue, and mosaics from as far away as Libya and Jordan depict it as the emblem of the city itself.

Legacy

The lighthouse gave its name to the architectural type: pharos became the word for lighthouse in Greek, and survives in the French phare, Italian and Spanish faro, Romanian far and Portuguese farol. Minarets of early Egyptian mosques are thought by some scholars to echo its three-stage form, and modern reconstructions of the building have been proposed several times, including a 2015 Egyptian plan to rebuild it near the original site.
